PROJECT OVERVIEW

Why the Orange County Children’s Book Festival?
The Orange County Children’s Book Festival’s current website does not successfully convey important information to new visitors.
How are we going to make the festival’s online presence more inviting?
Redesign the website responsively to attract new visitors and quickly provide them with the most important information.

USER RESEARCH

We started by talking to a few users.
We spoke to parents of young children, educators, potential volunteers, and potential donors to understand their relationships with nonprofit organizations and more specifically, their interactions on nonprofit web interfaces. Here are a few of the more interesting takeaways from our conversations:

User 1

“I need information quickly
if I don’t know what the
organization is”

User 2

“I don’t want to volunteer if it
will take too much time”

User 3

“Transparency in the non-profit organization
is important to me”
We then gave users a chance to see the current website for five seconds so we could understand how they viewed information. The users enjoyed the visibility of the dates of the festival but felt that the bring orange and blue color scheme was jarring and distracting. Many of them also disliked the placement of the donate button and the lack of explanation of how donations will help the organization.
We were also lucky enough to interview the CEO and Co-Founder of the whole enchilada.

Pat Burns cofounded the OC Children’s Book Festival a few years ago and, when we spoke with her, provided some fantastic insight to the motivation behind the festival. Speaking with her made our project personal; aside from getting a good grade on our project, we wanted to create a redesign that would bring to life Pat’s vision.
She explained that:
1. Acquiring sponsors and volunteers is the most difficult part of putting together the festival.
2. The festival helps donate over 200,000 books to developing countries, but the website doesn’t tell us this!
3. To communicate with visitors, donors, and volunteers, the organization uses Facebook rather than its own website. The current website reroutes interested parties to Facebook.

LOW FIDELITY WEB WIREFRAMING

Next, we sat down to draw out our low fidelity wireframes using everything we’ve learned about our users. We decided on a clean, simple design that clearly showed the most important features of the festival: the date, the headliners, and ways to donate or get involved. Many users we interviewed mentioned difficulty navigating the website, so we designed the home page to be easy and scrollable, with only necessary information.

LOW FIDELITY PROTOTYPING

The low fidelity wireframes translated to low fidelity prototypes we could test.

DESIGN CHOICES

After wireframing and testing with a few users, we settled on a few stylistic choices. Although the original website was shockingly bright orange and blue, we wanted to keep the vibrant and excited feel for which the original designers were (presumably) striving. We kept the feeling equally as bright but opted for a teal and darker orange theme.
We settled on the font family “Quicksand” because it is not only legible, but also youthful with the rounded edges and wide letters.
Arguably the team’s favorite part of the design is our mascots: the fun-loving green and purple dinosaurs that decorate each page of the redesign. Of high importance was incorporating a youthful element to the redesign because the event is indeed, young.

Color prototype testing and changes

Testing gave us some insight on how users would interact with our interface.
A few issues our users ran into were:

User 1

“Why am I being shown volunteer opportunities
before I even sign up?”

User 2

“This modal doesn’t tell me enough; will I receive an
email confirmation?”

User 3

“What am I supposed to
do after signing up or donating?”

Taking these issues into account, we flipped the volunteer form and opportunities to provide users time to sign up before
browsing events they can participate in.
We added more information on what donors’ money is funding in the popup window, reflecting on testers’ comments and
also previous interviewees who said they need transparency in a nonprofit before they would consider donating time or
money.
Instead of simply having the “Thank you” modal sit on top of the web page until a user clicked away, we added an option to
make another donation or exit to the home page.

Learning is a process

And we're always learning!

Reflecting on a project from start to finish is always so rewarding yet frustrating, because at a thousand points we stop and groan and pull out a couple fistfuls of hair. “Why didn’t we just do this?” “Why didn’t we ask this question?” “If we just moved everything a pixel to the left, it would be so much better…” In the throes of a project we’re convinced our vision is perfect; that just because the information architecture we spent so long card sorting makes sense to us, it makes sense to everyone else. But at the end of the day, we’re not really the user. Working on this project in particular really drove that idea home: I am not the user.

We had to rely so heavily on users because as designers, we were blind at times. Our most valuable insights came from the conversations we had with users, whether we were asking direct questions or completely tangenting into a different subject and taking notes on their thought processes. So I guess the two main takeaways are these: nothing is ever completely finished, and the user is (mainly) always right.
